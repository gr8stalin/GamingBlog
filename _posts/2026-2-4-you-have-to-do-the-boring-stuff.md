When I first got into fighting games, way before anyone could pigeonhole me into any specific fighting game, my og FPS/RTS community dabbled in fighting games on the side and I got a lesson with one of my homies from there. One of our most experienced went over blocking, plus and minus frames, and so on, but he told me something that hasn't left me to this day: "you practice the fundamentals until you die". It's been true every step of the way and everyone who has practiced something that requires practice all comes back to the same fact:

For Street Fighter 6, I've had a handful of coaching calls with [Shine](https://metafy.gg/@shinenyc) where he's seen some of my worst games. Outside of the SF6-specific stuff I've taken away from the calls, I've realized that every piece of guidance he's given just comes down to either "look over your fundamentals again" or "work on this fundamental because it's weak right now": throw people a lot until they start teching, hit your combos that give you oki, spend your meter for oki and/or damage, know when to take your turn back on defense.

An old friend has been struggling with the shift from Street Fighter 5 to Street Fighter 6, and after one battle lounge session we had I realized he was suffering from some of the same problems I had but to a more pronounced degree. Lots of his drive rush checks would simply result in me getting hit by the button with no knockdown, if he landed a light it'd just be a panic mash and not turned into a knockdown, he rarely threw me, and when I'd send unsafe stuff I'd often be left alive as he'd not spend his resources to kill me.

I shared my experience with him and pointed out where in one of our games the clearest cut examples were. Two days later, he stopped flailing around in Diamond and put his new character in Master.

The only way to get better at a skill-based passtime is to practice fundamental skills. Everything else is entertainment.
