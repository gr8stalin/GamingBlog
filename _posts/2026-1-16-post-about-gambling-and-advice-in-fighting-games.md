I think one of the aspects of fighting games that contributes to me placing it at the bottom of the 1v1 Triumverate is that a lot of mid~high ranking fighting game players think they're geniuses for offering platitudes as advice. I don't think it really matters what fighting game it is; even though the poster in question is a Tekken player, I see this in Street Fighter too, saw it in Granblue Versus Vanilla, etc. The piece of advice offered here is also a very common platitude: "just press when they don't expect it". Twitter discourse and chat conversation like this won't stop until the "platitude delivery" group changes their ways.

The advice being offered is extremely shallow and poorly framed, but an important part that's usually missing when talking about advice giving is that this shallow platitude is being proffered as deep wisdom. 

The anonymized player is clearly trying to improve their online ranking and is getting frustrated that their opponents are just pressing their own attack buttons after the player makes their opponent block a plus on block attack. Since that attack is plus on block, the anonymized player should be able to beat their opponent mashing with a button that has appropriate attributes. The player is more than likely not seeing this exchange play out in their favor and it's driving them up a wall.

Telling this player the secret to higher rank is "doing what isn't expected" (gambling) and framing it not only as a **complete** explanation for the player's woes (i.e., they are just guessing better than you) but also as some deep wisdom ("Tough to stomach but it's true") sounds both incredibly condescending *and* stupid. I think the anonymized player is fully within their right to call that platitude "the dumbest shit [they've ever] heard". It's entitled and immature to offer something that shallow and dismissive as advice and then go "lol i tried helping guess you'll never learn :)" when you're dismissed for it.

## Context for the contextless

From hereon out I expect you to know a bit about fighting game terminology. Doing the unexpected is about picking options, on offense and defense, that disrupt whatever strategy your opponent has based on the gameplay they've seen from you. When defending, maybe it's doing something like dashing up after blocking the opponent's blockstring because they're looking for something like a poke or jump, or maybe it's simply just mashing a fast button in the middle of the opponent's buttons because they're doing tick throws a lot. When attacking, sometimes it's just about swinging the same medium or heavy button at your opponent's block until they bite, or setting up a situation where you're minus and expect the opponent to retaliate so you go into an immediate invincible reversal or super.

"Doing what isn't expected" is a platitude: it may be true ***with context***, but it doesn't offer any guidance to fix any of the anonymized player's problems. There's nothing in the messages about options, and from what I know about Discord conversations like this, it either turned into more condescending garbage and aggressiveness or the conversation died there. You can tell me all about Romanjelly's "keep gambling" meme or whatever, but you can't use it to hid behind the truth of the matter; the platitude tribe do this contextless advice-giving constantly and then either retreat behind "it was a joke" or "it's real advice too bad you're not open to learning :)".

# Some thoughts on advice giving for fighting games

This post by Romanjelly perfectly captures what most fighting game players seem to view as real advice giving. Some things that the platitude tribe doesn't understand when they give platitudes as advice to strangers or unfamiliar acquaintances:

- If done over text, there's zero context available: 
    - only the player is witness to their own suffering, and clarity & honesty in reporting their experience is on them (they are an unreliable narrator)
    - only the advice giver is aware of how the platitude was eventually turned into actionable advice for them
- The advice giver is just as responsible for the words they type/speak as the recipient of the advice
    - If you as an advice giver have nothing but platitudes to contribute that you can't elaborate on, it is your responsibility to either empathize or shut up
    - If you are determined to give advice, you are responsible for not only demanding context but *not* reducing it to a "see [x] do [y]" platitude[^1]

I struggled for a long time to play this genre competently, and I had a lot of trouble making my way through the online matchmaking swamps when I was actively grinding for Street Fighter 5. Fortunately and possibly unlike the anonymized player, I had a lot of support between my friends circle that was actively playing SF5, Zaferino and his community Fighter Academy, and Espadah. It really was a blessing because I got to skip a lot of the bullshit I encountered when I was new and in the New Challenger Discord, I got to skip a lot of variations of the posts like Romanjelly's, and I got to see both sides of good and bad advice giving.

In retrospect, the least actionable advice I got was when I was describing situations over text. It's impossible to convey the minutiae of frame dependent interactions and positioning over text, all context is entirely dependent on what the author includes, and all solutions are based entirely on the asker's ability to envision the solution in their head. Example of the last point is spacing: in SF5, Alex elbow is punishable but not if it's spaced. Same with Balrog dash punch when it was buffed to be -4 on all version (huge mistake). The platitude was "you're on a 3f character, just use your 3f normal", but the real minutiae for making it punishable was altering your walk-and-block timings to bait either character into doing their yeet special so you could punish with your 3f normal.

One caveat to text-based advice is if the situation is clear cut enough, or the advice giver has proper context. In SF6, I was fortunate enough to play Banehollow (2k mr Marisa) for a few sets, and a perfect situation occurred where I got a platitude as advice but then actionable advice from his Twitch chat. The platitude tribe member basically said "you can whiff punish Marisa's superman lunge" and left it at that - in fighting games, you can whiff punish people. I felt like I was being patronized. The actionable advice chatter described how he as a Marisa player would get blown up for using Marisa's superman lunge, telling me to try walkup jab to get a juggle state, be ok with giving up screen space and trying to whiff punish when it was more clear a superman lunge was likely, or use walkback fireball to set up a dp punish with walkback do nothing. Take a guess as to which one actually helped me with that part of the matchup.

Submitting Sub Sunday[^1] replays to Zaferino was the middle ground in regards to actionable advice. Zaf would have the context of one game in a set, and that context would grow as you became a regular during Sub Sunday sessions. Some of the least actionable advice he gave was typically mentality related and it felt mostly useless[^2] when I was struggling in Gold rank. Some of the most actionable advice was when he was able to spot the mechanical deficiencies in my play and say "you should try doing [x] every time you run into [y] matchup to punish [z] - you may lose or may not even get to do [x] but it'll get you in the mindset of doing [x]". I don't think I really started consciously whiff punishing until a Sub Sunday replay review, and I consider that particular Sub Sunday a canon fighting game event for me.

The most actionable advice I got was when it was live. This little moment was one of my few good memories of Gold rank in SF5, where I got the valuable lesson of "you do not always have to participate in neutral and press buttons when they are pressing lots of buttons":

When you're being watched live, your hybrid viewer-coaches have some of the most context they're ever gonna be able to get from you. They see what your opponent's decision making is like, see what you're doing, see what you're *not* doing, and can huddle both live and after the fact. This is where platitudes fade away completely; they are functionally useless when presented with this level of context.

# Takeaways

This isn't something that'll be fixed in a month, or even a year, but I think the way people in fighting games give advice really, *really* needs to change. You guys are in a new era of gaming: people online aren't lesser beings because they're not at your local[^4]. There's nothing "soft" about being a reasonable human being if someone you don't know but is proximal to you is having a rough time.

Community has been online in other genres for a long time and fighting game players are just in time to experience over 30 years of infrastructure come together in things like Steam and Discord.

Own your advice: if you're in a chat, you can ask clarifying questions and see if you can actually offer anything substantial, or if that person is even willing to engage with you. Don't just spit something out and go "lol guess you aren't open minded :)" in passive-aggressive rage when the recipient realizes they're not being treated fairly or normally. Maybe the best thing to do is just empathize with the other person; starting with empathy and creating a shared enemy (i.e., online mashers) will give you an opportunity to find out their real problem.
